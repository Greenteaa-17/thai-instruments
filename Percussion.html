Percussion Instrument

Thai Percussion Instruments (เครื่องตีของไทย)Thai percussion instruments are instruments that produce sound by striking, hitting, or shaking. They are the rhythmic and energetic foundation of Thai classical music, especially in the Piphat (ปี่พาทย์) ensemble.

Main Types of Thai Percussion Instruments

Taphon (ตะโพน)

  • Barrel-shaped drum, played with hands
  • Considered the leading drum in the Piphat ensemble
  • Used to signal rhythm and control tempo

Klong That (กลองทัด)

  • Pair of large drums, played with sticks
  • Deep, powerful sound
  • Often used in ceremonies and processions

Klong Song Na (กลองสองหน้า)

  • Double-headed drum
  • Played with hands or sticks
  • Provides rhythmic support in many ensembles

Klong Khaek (กลองแขก)

  • A pair of hand drums with Indian/Malay influence
  • Used for fast and lively rhythms

Ching (ฉิ่ง)

  • Small pair of cup-shaped cymbals
  • Keep the basic rhythmic cycle (Ching-Chap pattern)
  • Essential in almost every Thai ensemble

Chab (ฉาบ)

  • Larger cymbals than Ching
  • Produce louder, crashing sounds
  • Used to emphasize accents

Krap (กรับ)

  • Wooden or bamboo clappers
  • Used to mark rhythm and accents

Ranat Ek (ระนาดเอก)

  • Wooden xylophone with bars over a resonating box
  • Produces bright, sharp melodic tones
  • Plays the main melody in Piphat ensemble

Ranat Thum (ระนาดทุ้ม)

  • Lower-pitched xylophone, complements Ranat Ek
  • Provides harmonic and melodic support

Khong Wong Yai (ฆ้องวงใหญ่)

  • Large gong circle with tuned bossed gongs
  • Plays the main melody line
  • Important instrument in Piphat ensemble

Khong Wong Lek (ฆ้องวงเล็ก)

  • Smaller gong circle, higher pitch than Khong Wong Yai
  • Plays variations and ornaments of the melody

Characteristics of Thai Percussion Instruments

  1. Provide both rhythm (drums, cymbals, clappers) and melody (ranat, gongs)
  2. Very important in Piphat ensemble, where percussion dominates
  3. Create the energy, dynamics, and atmosphere of Thai classical music
